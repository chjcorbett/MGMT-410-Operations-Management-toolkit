Process · 01 · Medium
ColdBrew Café — Build-to-Order Beverages
BottleneckCapacityUtilization

ColdBrew Café makes specialty drinks in a four-step serial process with one worker per step.

Figure 1 — ColdBrew process. Gold border = bottleneck step (★).
a)

What is the bottleneck cycle time (min/drink)?

The bottleneck is the step that sets the pace for the whole process. Which step takes the longest per drink?
List the task times: Grind=3, Milk=5, Blend=7, Label=2. The bottleneck is the step with the largest value. With one worker per step, CT = task time directly.
$CT_{\text{step}} = \text{Task time} \div \text{Workers}$. Here each step has 1 worker, so $CT = $ task time. Bottleneck $CT = \max(3, 5, 7, 2) = 7$ min/drink.
b)

What is the process capacity in drinks per hour?

Capacity = how many units the process can produce per hour. Which step limits output?
$\text{Capacity} = 60 \div CT_{\text{bottleneck}}$. You found the bottleneck CT in part (a).
c)

What is the utilization of the Grind & Brew worker (Step A)?

Enter as a percentage, e.g. 65
Utilization = flow rate ÷ step capacity. All steps in a serial process run at the same flow rate (the bottleneck rate).
Flow rate = 1/CT_bottleneck. Step A capacity = 1/CT_A. Utilization = CT_A ÷ CT_bottleneck.
$u_{\text{step}} = CT_{\text{step}} \div CT_{\text{bottleneck}} = 3 \div 7$
d)

Drinks sell for $6 with $1.50 material cost; workers earn $18/hr. In an 8-hour shift with demand exceeding capacity, what is the daily profit? (Round to nearest dollar.)

Negative = loss
Three components: Revenue, Material cost, Labor cost. Output per shift = capacity × shift hours.
Output = floor(8.57 × 8) = 68 drinks. Revenue = 68 × $6. Materials = 68 × $1.50. Labor = 4 workers × $18 × 8 hrs.
$\pi = (\$6.00 - \$1.50) \times 68 - 4 \times \$18 \times 8 = \$306 - \$576 = -\$270$

Complete Solution

a–c) Cycle times (task ÷ workers): A=3, B=5, C=7 (bottleneck), D=2 min/drink.

Capacity = 60 / 7 = 8.57 drinks/hr Util(A) = 3 / 7 = 42.9%

d) Output per shift = ⌊8.57 × 8⌋ = 68 drinks.

Revenue: 68 × $6.00 = $408.00 Materials: 68 × $1.50 = $102.00 Labor: 4 × $18 × 8 = $576.00 ──────────────────────────────────── Profit = 408 − 102 − 576 = −$270 (loss)

Despite running at full capacity, the narrow $4.50 margin cannot cover four workers earning $576/shift.

Current process capacity (units/hr)?

Capacity of each step = (workers × 3600) ÷ task time in seconds. The step with the lowest capacity is the bottleneck.
Two tied bottleneck steps (★): C has CT = 36/2 = 18 s/unit, E has CT = 18/1 = 18 s/unit. Capacity = 3600 / 18 = 200 units/hr.
b)

One worker moves from Step A to Step C. What is the new capacity (units/hr)?

Moving a worker from a non-bottleneck step to a bottleneck may or may not help — it depends on whether you create a new bottleneck elsewhere.
After move: A has 1 worker (CT=20s), C has 3 workers (CT=12s). Recalculate all CTs and find the new bottleneck.
c)

With all 10 workers fully cross-trained (can work any step), what is the maximum capacity (units/hr)? Round to 1 decimal.

With fully flexible workers, the bottleneck disappears. Think of it as a pool of labor: 10 workers × 60 min each = 600 worker-minutes per hour. Each unit needs the total of all task times.
Capacity = total worker-minutes per hour ÷ total task time per unit. Make sure units are consistent (minutes throughout).
$\text{Capacity} = \dfrac{N_{\text{workers}} \times 3600}{\sum \text{Task times}} = \dfrac{10 \times 3600}{20+45+36+30+18}$

a) CTs: A=10, B=15, C=18★, D=15, E=18★ (tied bottleneck) Capacity = 3600/18 = 200 units/hr b) Move 1 worker A→C: A now 1 worker (CT=20s), C now 3 (CT=12s) New CTs: A=20★, B=15, C=12, D=15, E=18 New bottleneck: A (20s) New capacity = 3600/20 = 180 units/hr ← DECREASED! c) Total task time = 20+45+36+30+18 = 149 s Flexible capacity = (10×3600)/149 = 241.6 units/hr

Key insight: moving a worker to the bottleneck can create a new bottleneck elsewhere and actually hurt performance.

Process · 05 · Medium
Metro Deli — Little's Law in Reverse
Little's LawWIPThroughput

Metro Deli runs a fast-lunch assembly line. A manager observes:

  • Average of 8 customers in the process (from order to pickup)
  • Average customer spends 12 minutes from order to pickup
a)

What is the throughput rate (customers/hr)?

Apply Little's Law: flow rate $A = L / W$. You know the average number of customers in the system ($L$) and their average time in the system ($W$).
$A = L / W$. Convert $W$ from minutes to hours first, then compute $A$ in customers/hr.
$A = L / W = 8 \text{ customers} / (12/60 \text{ hr}) = 8 / 0.2 = 40$ customers/hr
b)

Management installs a new sandwich toaster that cuts average time to 9 minutes. If throughput stays at 40/hr, how many customers are now in the system on average?

Use Little's Law: $L = A \times W$. Convert $W$ to hours before multiplying by the hourly arrival rate.
$L = 40 \text{ customers/hr} \times (9/60 \text{ hr}) = ?$ customers.
c)

The deli adds an express lane and throughput jumps to 60/hr with W still 12 minutes. New WIP (customers in system)?

Use Little's Law: $L = A \times W$ with the new arrival rate and $W$ converted to hours.

Complete Solution

a) A = L/W = 8 / (12/60) = 40 customers/hr b) L = A × W = 40 × (9/60) = 6 customers c) L = A × W = 60 × (12/60) = 12 customers

Little's Law is powerful precisely because it requires no assumptions about distributions — it holds for any stable system.

BuildRight Construction — Uncertain Durations
UncertaintyExpected Duration

BuildRight's critical path A→B→C→D→E has a deterministic length of 30 weeks. This baseline assumes Activity C (Framing) takes its standard duration of 8 weeks. However, C's actual duration is uncertain:

ScenarioDurationProbability
Standard8 wks50%
Weather delay11 wks30%
Favorable6 wks20%

Activity D (MEP) has a 40% chance of a rework loop adding 3 weeks.

a)

What is E[C], the expected duration of Activity C (weeks)?

The expected value of a discrete random variable is the probability-weighted average of all outcomes. Multiply each duration by its probability, then add them up.
Three scenarios for C: 8 wks (p=0.50), 11 wks (p=0.30), 6 wks (p=0.20). Compute (8×0.50) + (11×0.30) + (6×0.20).
$E[C] = 0.50 \times 8 + 0.30 \times 11 + 0.20 \times 6 = 4.0 + 3.3 + 1.2 = 8.5$ weeks.
b)

Expected project duration (weeks)?

The baseline (30 wks) uses C=8 and no rework. Two sources of extra time: C being above 8 wks, and D rework.
Extra from C = E[C] − 8 wks. Extra from D = 0.40 × 3 wks. Add both to the 30-week baseline.
$E[\text{project}] = 30 + (8.5 - 8) + (0.4 \times 3) = 30 + 0.5 + 1.2 = 31.7$ wks
c)

Probability project finishes in ≤ 30 weeks? (Enter as a percentage, e.g., 42 for 42%)

The baseline uses C = 8 and no D rework. Finishing in ≤ 30 weeks requires the project not to exceed the baseline. List all scenario combinations where no extra time is added.
Finish ≤ 30 requires C ≤ 8 wks AND no D rework. P(C ≤ 8) = P(C=8) + P(C=6) = 0.50 + 0.20 = 0.70. P(no rework) = 0.60. Since C and D are independent: P = 0.70 × 0.60 = 0.42 = 42%.

Complete Solution

a) E[C] = 0.5×8 + 0.3×11 + 0.2×6 = 4.0+3.3+1.2 = 8.5 wks b) E[project] = 30 + (8.5−8) + (0.4×3) = 30+0.5+1.2 = 31.7 wks c) Finish ≤30 requires C≤8 AND no rework: P = P(C=8)×P(no rework) + P(C=6)×P(no rework) = (0.5+0.2)×0.6 = 42% (C=6 with rework: 30−2+3=31 > 30, so that doesn't qualify)

Queueing Theory

Utilization, queue length, waiting time, variability, server pooling

📐 Core Queueing Formulas — Quick Reference
Step 1 — Utilization
$$u = \frac{A}{m \cdot S}$$
$A$ = arrival rate, $S$ = service rate per server, $m$ = servers. Need $u < 1$.
Step 2 — Queue Length $L_q$
$$L_q = \frac{u^{\,\sqrt{2(m+1)}}}{1-u} \cdot \frac{CV_{\!IAT}^2 + CV_{\!ST}^2}{2}$$
M/M/1 shortcut (both CV=1): $L_q = u^2/(1-u)$
Step 3 — Wait & System Time
$$W_q = \frac{L_q}{A} \qquad W = W_q + \frac{1}{S}$$
$W_q$ = wait in queue only. Add $1/S$ for total time including service.

Question 1 2 pts
In a balanced serial process with five steps and one worker per step, which change will increase process capacity WITHOUT affecting throughput time?
Think about what 'balanced' means — every step is equally the bottleneck.
Throughput time = sum of per-unit task times. Adding workers doesn't change how long each step takes for one unit — it allows parallel processing. In a balanced process, adding workers to only one step doesn't break the tie.

Explanation

Answer: (d). In a balanced process all five steps are co-bottlenecks. Adding a second worker to every step doubles each step's capacity, including all bottlenecks, so process capacity doubles. Throughput time is unchanged because each unit still spends the same task time at each step. Options (a)–(c) either don't increase capacity or have side effects on throughput time.

Question 2 2 pts
An outpatient clinic has an average of 45 patients present at any one time. On average, patients spend 3 hours at the clinic. Using Little's Law, what is the average patient arrival rate?

Rearrange to $A = L / W$. Make sure units match: $L = 45$ patients, $W = 3$ hours.
$A = L / W = 45 / 3 = 15$ patients/hour.

Explanation

Answer: (a). $A = L/W = 45 \div 3 = 15$ patients/hour. Common error: option (b) multiplies instead of dividing.

A hospital has two independent triage stations each serving half of all patients. A consultant proposes consolidating into a single pooled queue served by both nurses. Compared to the original, the pooled system will have:

Answer: (b). Utilization is unchanged (same total capacity and arrivals). But waiting time strictly decreases: pooling eliminates the inefficiency where one queue is long while another server is idle. This is a fundamental result in queueing theory.

Question Q2 — GreenWave Coffee (Queueing) 9 pts

GreenWave Coffee has one barista station. Customers arrive at 20/hr ($CV_a=1.0$). Average service time = 2.5 min/customer ($CV_s=1.0$).

a) [3 pts]

What is the utilization of the barista station? Show your work.

A = 20 customers/hr. Service time = 2.5 min/customer → S = 60 ÷ 2.5 = 24 customers/hr.

Utilization: u = A ÷ (m × S) = 20 ÷ (1 × 24) = 0.833 = 83.3%.

The system is stable (u < 1). Each barista is busy about 83% of the time.

b) [3 pts]

GreenWave is considering adding a second barista who would share the same queue. Qualitatively, why would average waiting time fall by MORE than 50%?

Two compounding reasons:

1. Utilization drops sharply — and the effect is nonlinear. With 2 servers: u = 20/(2×24) = 0.417, vs 0.833 before. Wait is a convex function of u. Moving from 0.833 (steep part) to 0.417 (flat part) produces a disproportionately large drop — far more than 50%.

2. Pooling eliminates stranded idle capacity. With two separate queues, one barista can be idle while the other has a line. With a shared queue, a barista is idle only when no customer is waiting at all. This further reduces average wait beyond what the utilization drop alone predicts.

c) [3 pts]

A customer arrives and finds 3 customers ahead; the first of those 3 is just starting service. Use Little's Law to estimate this customer's wait before beginning service.

The arriving customer must wait for all 3 ahead to complete service.

Using Little's Law: treat the 3 customers as inventory $L = 3$ flowing through the server at rate $S = 24$/hr.

W = L / S = 3 / 24 hr = 0.125 hr = 7.5 minutes.

Alternatively: 3 × 2.5 min/customer = 7.5 min. Both methods confirm the same answer.

c) [3 pts]

If activity durations are uncertain and independent, will expected project duration be greater than, equal to, or less than 27 weeks? Justify.

Greater than 27 weeks.

Project duration = max(Path 1, Path 2). The expected value of the maximum of two random variables ≥ maximum of their expected values: $E[\max(X,Y)] \geq \max(E[X], E[Y])$.

With deterministic expected durations both equal 27, project = exactly 27. With random durations, any realization where one path exceeds 27 delays the project — and this is NOT offset by cases where both are short (we take the max, not the average).

This is called merge point bias: projects consistently run longer than nominal. Build schedule buffers, especially when multiple paths are close in length.

b) [3 pts]

A fast-food chain is considering converting its three drive-through lanes (dedicated cashiers) into a single pooled queue served by three cashiers. What are the key operational benefits and potential drawbacks?

Benefits (pooling theorem from queueing theory):

1. Lower average wait time — idle capacity is never stranded in one lane while another is backed up. Utilization per cashier is unchanged but Wq falls.

2. No 'wrong lane' regret — the single queue is fair and removes the variance in individual customer experience.

Potential drawbacks:

1. Physical layout constraints — a single merging lane requires more space and careful traffic engineering for a drive-through.

2. Perceived queue length — a long single line may deter customers who see it, even if actual wait time is shorter.

3. If cars can't freely choose among cashiers (committed to a lane before reaching the cashier), the pooling benefit may be partially lost.

c) [3 pts]

A grocery retailer orders its best-selling cereal twice a year in large bulk shipments. A supply chain consultant recommends smaller, more frequent orders. What does the EOQ model predict? What assumptions drive this prediction?

EOQ prediction: Ordering twice a year almost certainly puts the current Q far above the EOQ, so holding costs greatly exceed ordering costs. Moving toward smaller, more frequent orders (closer to EOQ) will reduce total annual cost by lowering average inventory more than it raises total ordering cost.

Key assumptions: (1) Demand is known and approximately constant — no strong seasonality. (2) Ordering and holding cost parameters are stable. (3) No quantity discounts for bulk purchases (otherwise bulk may still be optimal). (4) No supplier minimum order constraints.

Caveat: if bulk orders earn significant per-unit discounts, the retailer should compare total cost including purchase price — the discount may justify large batches even against EOQ logic.

d) [3 pts]

A ski resort must decide before the season how many daily lift passes to produce (printed in advance, cannot be reused). Frame this as a newsvendor problem and explain the ordering decision.

The incremental cost of serving one more skier is essentially zero — the lift is running anyway.

e) [3 pts]

Toyota's TPS emphasizes making defects visible immediately rather than discovering them at the end of a batch. Why is early defect detection so valuable from an operations perspective?

Three compounding advantages:

1. Prevents waste accumulation. If a defect isn't caught until end of a run, all labor, material, and time invested after the defect occurred is wasted. Catching it immediately stops the waste at unit one (jidoka principle).

2. Preserves root-cause information. Defects are easiest to diagnose while fresh. With small batches and immediate feedback, the settings, worker actions, or material lot that caused the problem are still identifiable. In large batches processed hours later, causal information is lost.

3. Limits the number of defective units. A defect caught after unit 1 affects 1 unit. A defect caught at end of a 500-unit batch potentially contaminates all 500. Small batches and in-process checks minimize escape of defects downstream.

Question 2 — Queueing Analysis: ClinicFlow Urgent Care (9 points)
Question Q2 — ClinicFlow Urgent Care 9 pts

ClinicFlow Urgent Care has 3 exam rooms (each staffed by one physician). Patients arrive at 30/hr ($CV_a = 1.0$). Each consultation averages 4 minutes ($CV_s = 1.0$).

a) [3 pts]

What is the utilization of each exam room?

Arrival rate: $A = 30$ patients/hr.

Service rate per room: $S = 60 / 4 = 15$ patients/hr.

Utilization: $u = A / (m \times S) = 30 / (3 \times 15) = 30/45 = 2/3 \approx 66.7\%$.

The system is stable ($u < 1$). Each physician is busy about two-thirds of the time.

b) [3 pts]

ClinicFlow is considering opening a 4th exam room. What will happen to (i) utilization per room and (ii) average patient waiting time? Be precise about direction and relative magnitude.

(i) Utilization with 4 rooms: $u = 30/(4 \times 15) = 30/60 = 50\%$. Utilization drops from 66.7% to 50% — a reduction of one-quarter.

(ii) Average waiting time drops by much more than one-quarter. Waiting time is a highly nonlinear (convex) function of utilization — the curve grows steeply as $u$ approaches 1. Moving from 66.7% to 50% shifts the system from a relatively steep portion of the curve to a much flatter portion, producing a disproportionately large reduction in $W_q$. The fourth room also adds server pooling benefits.

c) [3 pts]

A consultant proposes standardizing consultations to reduce $CV_s$ from 1.0 to 0.5 (mean service time unchanged). What effect will this have on average waiting time?

Average waiting time will decrease.

The queueing approximation shows $W_q \propto (CV_a^2 + CV_s^2)/2$.

Before: $V = (1^2 + 1^2)/2 = 1.0$. After reducing $CV_s$ to 0.5: $V = (1^2 + 0.5^2)/2 = 1.25/2 = 0.625$.

$W_q$ falls by $(1 - 0.625/1.0) = $ 37.5%. Utilization is unchanged (mean service time is the same), so only the V term changes. This illustrates that variability — not just capacity — is a key driver of wait time.

Which step is the bottleneck in LuminaPack?

Bottleneck = $\arg\min$(capacity) = Step B at 2.4 u/hr.

Question 5 — Miscellaneous Open-Text Questions (21 points)
Question Q5 — Miscellaneous Open-Text Questions 21 pts

Each sub-question is worth 3 points. Write your answer, then reveal the sample solution.

a) [3 pts]

If managing your email inbox is your personal bottleneck, describe three strategies to manage it better. Link each explicitly to a course concept.

1. Schedule two fixed 'email blocks' per day → Batch processing / EOQ setup-cost logic. Switching between email and focused work has a high cognitive setup cost. Batching email into fixed slots reduces the number of switches and amortizes setup time — fewer batches, lower total cost.

2. Use filters to route low-priority emails into folders → Bottleneck management / priority queueing. Protect the bottleneck (your focused attention) from low-priority interruptions. Only high-value items pass through the bottleneck resource.

3. Apply a WIP limit: never leave more than N emails active → Little's Law / WIP control. By $L = A \times W$, keeping $L$ (active items) small reduces $W$ (average time to resolution per email), making the system more responsive.

b) [3 pts]

A manager argues: 'Our agents are idle 20% of the time, so we can cut the workforce by 10% — utilization will rise from 80% to 89%, saving 10% on labor.' Is this analysis correct? What are the risks?

The analysis is not correct. The logic that a 10% workforce cut causes only a small service impact is flawed because waiting time is a highly nonlinear function of utilization.

At $u = 0.80$ the queue is already moderately long. Moving from $u = 0.80$ to $u = 0.89$ moves along the steep (exponential) portion of the $W_q$ vs. $u$ curve. The increase in wait time will be far larger than 10% — possibly 50–100% or more depending on the number of servers and variability.

The manager is confusing a resource efficiency metric (idle time) with a system performance metric (wait time). The nonlinear relationship between utilization and wait time makes these very different things.

c) [3 pts]

Hospital Alpha has highly predictable patient volume (low CV). Hospital Beta serves a walk-in population with highly unpredictable arrivals (high CV). Which hospital should maintain a larger capacity buffer (operate at lower utilization), and why?

Hospital Beta (high CV) should maintain the larger capacity buffer and operate at lower utilization.

The queueing approximation shows $W_q \propto (CV_a^2 + CV_s^2)/2$. For the same utilization $u$, a system with higher arrival variability produces a much longer queue.

To achieve the same waiting time target as Alpha, Beta must compensate by operating at lower utilization — leaving more idle capacity as a buffer against demand spikes.

Intuition: if arrivals are predictable, you can staff precisely for expected load. If arrivals are unpredictable, spikes will overwhelm tight staffing, so you need slack. This is the fundamental trade-off between efficiency (high utilization) and responsiveness (low variability / low wait time).

d) [3 pts]

A clothing retailer orders its flagship jeans twice a year in large shipments. A supply chain analyst recommends monthly orders. What does the EOQ model predict about total annual inventory costs? What assumptions must hold?

EOQ prediction: Ordering twice a year likely means current Q >> EOQ, so holding costs greatly exceed ordering costs. Moving toward smaller, more frequent orders (closer to EOQ) will reduce total annual cost by lowering average inventory more than it raises total ordering cost.

Key assumptions that must hold: (1) Demand is approximately constant and known — no strong seasonality. (2) Ordering and holding cost parameters are stable. (3) No quantity discounts for bulk purchases (if large orders earn significant per-unit discounts, the EOQ analysis understates the true cost of ordering frequently). (4) No minimum order quantity constraints from the supplier.

e) [3 pts]

A concert venue must decide how many VIP parking passes to print before each show. Passes are printed in advance; any unsold passes are worthless. Frame this as a newsvendor problem. Which cost dominates, and what does this imply for optimal quantity?

Newsvendor framing: $C_o$ = printing cost per pass (paid whether or not sold). $C_u$ = revenue lost per unsold opportunity = ticket premium − printing cost (foregone margin per potential buyer).

Which dominates: $C_u$ almost certainly dominates, since ticket premium is typically much larger than printing cost. CF = $C_u/(C_u+C_o) > 0.50$.

Implication: The venue should overprint rather than underprint. A few unsold passes (small printing cost wasted) is a smaller loss than turning away paying premium customers. The exact $Q^*$ depends on the revenue-to-cost ratio and demand distribution.

f) [3 pts]

Toyota's 'andon cord' allows any worker to signal a problem that can stop part or all of the production line. A skeptic argues stopping a line producing thousands of units per day is enormously expensive. How would you respond?

The skeptic's framing is wrong in two ways:

First, the cost of NOT stopping is higher. If a defect is not caught immediately, it propagates downstream: subsequent steps add labor and materials to a flawed unit, and potentially hundreds of defective units accumulate. Stopping at unit 1 is far cheaper than scrapping or reworking hundreds.

Second, pulling the cord forces root-cause resolution. A flagged problem must be addressed before the affected station resumes — there is no 'deal with it later.' Critically, the cord does not always stop the entire line immediately: a team leader is first alerted and may resolve the issue within a fixed time window; only if unresolved does the stop propagate. Over time, recurring stoppages decrease as root causes are eliminated, and plants using andon systems aggressively tend to have higher long-run throughput and lower defect rates.

g) [3 pts]

The 'Good Jobs Strategy' argues paying workers well and investing in their training improves operational performance — not just morale. Explain the operational logic.

The Good Jobs Strategy rests on a self-reinforcing operational logic:

1. Lower turnover → lower training/hiring costs, more experienced workers, fewer errors. In service operations, experienced workers are faster, create less rework, handle exceptions better — all reduce variability ($CV_s$) and raise throughput.

2. Higher engagement → workers identify and fix process problems (kaizen), follow procedures consistently, and catch defects early (jidoka). Workers who feel disposable have no incentive to improve the process they will soon leave.

3. Better operations → higher revenue → ability to afford good wages. This closes the loop: good operations fund good wages, which sustain good operations. Key insight: low wages do NOT necessarily produce low labor costs once turnover, training, errors, and lost customers are accounted for.

Formula Sheet

All formulas typeset with KaTeX — suitable for printing as your exam equation sheet.

Process Analysis

Cycle Time
$$CT_{\text{step}} = \frac{\text{Task time}}{\text{Workers at step}}$$
In units of time/unit. Bottleneck = step with the largest CT. All workers at a step process units in parallel.
Capacity & Utilization
$$\text{Capacity} = \frac{1}{CT_{\text{BN}}} \qquad u_i = \frac{CT_i}{CT_{\text{BN}}}$$
BN = bottleneck. Utilization of any non-bottleneck < 100%. Only reducing bottleneck CT increases process capacity.
Little's Law
$$L = A \cdot W$$
$L$ = avg items in system; $A$ = throughput rate (arrival rate at steady state); $W$ = avg time in system. Valid for any stable system — no distributional assumptions required.

Project Management

Project Duration
$$T_\text{project} = \max_{\text{all paths}} \sum_{j \in \text{path}} d_j$$
Enumerate every path from start to finish, sum durations along each, and take the maximum. That longest path is the critical path.
Slack (Float)
$$\text{Slack}_j = T_\text{project} - \text{longest path through } j$$
An activity's slack = how much it can be delayed without delaying the project. Critical path activities have Slack = 0. Non-critical activities have Slack > 0.
Critical Path Method
$$\text{CP: activities where Slack} = 0$$
Only accelerating activities on the critical path shortens the project. After each acceleration, re-check: new critical paths may emerge as other paths "catch up."
Uncertainty (Jensen's Inequality)
$$E[T_{\text{project}}] \;\geq\; T_{\text{CP, deterministic}}$$
Because $E[\max] \geq \max[E]$. Multiple near-critical paths amplify the upward bias. Never use the deterministic CP length as your project ETA.

Queueing Theory

Utilization
$$u = \frac{A}{m \cdot S}$$
$A$ = arrival rate, $S$ = service rate per server, $m$ = number of servers. Requires $u < 1$ for a stable queue. The relationship between $u$ and waiting is highly nonlinear.
General Formulas (M/G/m)
$$L_q = \frac{u^{\sqrt{2(m+1)}}}{1-u} \cdot \frac{CV_{IAT}^2 + CV_{ST}^2}{2}$$
$$W_q = \frac{L_q}{A} \qquad W = W_q + \frac{1}{S} \qquad L = A \cdot W$$
$A$ = arrival rate, $S$ = service rate per server, $m$ = servers. The exponent $\sqrt{2(m+1)}$ grows with $m$, so pooling servers dramatically reduces $L_q$.
M/M/1 Simplification ($m{=}1$, both CV${=}1$)
$$L_q = \frac{u^2}{1-u} \qquad W_q = \frac{L_q}{A}$$
$$W = \frac{1}{S - A} \qquad L = A \cdot W$$
When $m=1$ and both $CV_{IAT}=CV_{ST}=1$ (CV_IAT = CV_ST = 1), these closed-form expressions replace the general formula. Near $u=1$, $L_q$ blows up nonlinearly.
Little's Law Applied to Queues
$$W_q = \frac{L_q}{A} \qquad W = W_q + \frac{1}{S}$$
$$L = A \cdot W \qquad L_q = A \cdot W_q$$
Little's Law links all four quantities. $W_q$ = wait in queue only; $W$ = total time including service.

Inventory Management

Economic Order Quantity
$$Q^* = \sqrt{\frac{2KD}{h}}$$
$K$ = ordering cost, $D$ = annual demand, $h$ = holding cost ($/unit/yr). At $Q^*$, annual holding cost = annual ordering cost (crossover point).
EOQ Sensitivity
$$Q^* \propto \sqrt{K}, \quad Q^* \propto \sqrt{D}, \quad Q^* \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{h}}$$
EOQ is robust: 4× change in any parameter shifts $Q^*$ by only 2×. This is why lean production invests in setup reduction — $K \to K/4$ enables $Q^* \to Q^*/2$.
Reorder Point (ROP)
$$ROP = \mu \cdot LT + \underbrace{z \cdot \sigma \cdot \sqrt{LT}}_{\text{safety stock}}$$
$\mu$ = avg demand/period, $LT$ = lead time (same units), $z$ = service-level z-score. Safety stock $\propto \sqrt{LT}$ — halving $LT$ saves 29%, not 50%.
Newsvendor — Critical Fractile
$$C_u = p - c \quad C_o = c - s \quad CF = \frac{C_u}{C_u + C_o}$$
$$Q^* = \mu + z(CF) \cdot \sigma$$
$p$ = price, $c$ = cost, $s$ = salvage. $P(\text{stockout}) = 1 - CF$. High $C_u$ relative to $C_o$ pushes $Q^*$ above the mean.
Risk Pooling
$$\sigma_{\text{pool}} = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n \sigma_i^2}$$
For $n$ i.i.d. streams: $\sigma_{\text{pool}} = \sigma\sqrt{n}$. Pooling $n$ locations reduces total SS from $n \cdot z\sigma$ to $z\sigma\sqrt{n}$ — a factor of $\sqrt{n}$ savings.
